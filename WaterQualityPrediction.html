1. Dataset and Task¶

1.1¶

Dataset Introduction - Water quality is a critical issue for public health and environmental safety. Contaminated water can lead to numerous health hazards, including disease outbreaks and long-term exposure to toxic substances. Assessing water safety based on the presence of chemical and biological contaminants is essential to prevent health risks. The dataset includes 7999 samples and 21 features, each representing different chemical components or biological contaminants in the water. The dataset is designed for a binary classification task, where the goal is to predict whether a water sample is safe or unsafe based on the concentrations of various chemical and biological elements.

Source: This is an imaginary data of water quality hosted on Kaggle. Link: https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/mssmartypants/water-quality

Dataset Size: 7999 samples and 21 features.

Features: The dataset contains 21 features, each representing the concentration levels (in mg/L) of various chemicals, heavy metals, or biological contaminants:

  • aluminium: Dangerous if greater than 2.8 mg/L.
  • ammonia: Dangerous if greater than 32.5 mg/L.
  • arsenic: Dangerous if greater than 0.01 mg/L.
  • barium: Dangerous if greater than 2 mg/L.
  • cadmium: Dangerous if greater than 0.005 mg/L.
  • chloramine: Dangerous if greater than 4 mg/L.
  • chromium: Dangerous if greater than 0.1 mg/L.
  • copper: Dangerous if greater than 1.3 mg/L.
  • flouride: Dangerous if greater than 1.5 mg/L.
  • bacteria: Dangerous if present in any amount.
  • viruses: Dangerous if present in any amount.
  • lead: Dangerous if greater than 0.015 mg/L.
  • nitrates: Dangerous if greater than 10 mg/L.
  • nitrites: Dangerous if greater than 1 mg/L.
  • mercury: Dangerous if greater than 0.002 mg/L.
  • perchlorate: Dangerous if greater than 56 mg/L.
  • radium: Dangerous if greater than 5 mg/L.
  • selenium: Dangerous if greater than 0.5 mg/L.
  • silver: Dangerous if greater than 0.1 mg/L.
  • uranium: Dangerous if greater than 0.3 mg/L.
  • is_safe (Target):

0: Unsafe (water sample exceeds one or more dangerous thresholds). 1: Safe (water sample is within safe concentration levels).

Known Challenges/limitations:

  • Domain Knowledge Integration: Proper understanding of water quality standards and the significance of each chemical's concentration is essential. Without environmental science expertise, it would be challenging to build models that maintain physical plausibility regarding safety thresholds or toxicity levels.

  • Handling Outliers: Some extreme values might represent unusual water samples (rare contamination) or errors. For example, high levels of a particular chemical may be due to data entry errors or rare pollution events. These need to be carefully handled to ensure the model's predictions are not skewed.

1.2¶

About the target variable For the purpose of our predictive classification task, the target variable is 'is_safe' (measured as 1: water is safe and 0: water is unsafe). It is a binary classification task.

Rationale Water quality is a critical factor for public health. Unsafe water can cause diseases and long-term health problems. Accurately predicting whether water is safe based on chemical concentrations can help in monitoring and ensuring water quality in public water supplies.

Relevant feaures

  1. Chemical Contaminants

These are primarily chemical elements or compounds that can pose a health hazard when they exceed safe concentration levels. This includes - Metals: Aluminium, Arsenic, Barium, Cadmium, Chromium, Copper, Lead, Mercury, Selenium, Silver, Uranium. Non-metals: Ammonia, Chloramine, Fluoride, Perchlorate.

  1. Biological Contaminants

These refer to living organisms or substances derived from living organisms that can cause diseases or infections if present in the water. This includes - Bacteria, Viruses.

  1. Inorganic Contaminants

These are inorganic compounds, commonly found in fertilizers and other industrial waste, that can be harmful to health. This includes - Nitrates, Nitrites.

  1. Radioactive Contaminants

These are naturally occurring or man-made radioactive elements that pose a significant health risk when consumed through water. This includes - Radium

  1. Pollutants from Industrial and Agricultural Sources

These are chemicals that are by-products of industrial and agricultural processes. This includes - Perchlorate

Potential challenges

  • Feature Correlation (Multicollinearity): Some features, particularly those related to heavy metals or chemical compounds, may exhibit high correlations, leading to multicollinearity. This could affect the interpretability and stability of the model. Correlation analysis will be necessary to identify these relationships.

  • ⁠Non-linearity: The relationships between certain chemical concentrations and water safety may not be strictly linear. For example, low concentrations of certain chemicals might be safe, but even a small increase could make the water hazardous. Advanced modeling techniques such as decision trees, random forests, or non-linear SVMs may be needed to capture these complexities.

  • Data Imbalance: There may be an imbalance between safe and unsafe water samples. If most of the water samples are safe, the model may become biased toward predicting the majority class, potentially overlooking unsafe water samples.

Relevance and Interest

If we are successful in predicting water quality and safety accurately, it could revolutionize how water treatment processes are managed in industries and municipalities dependent on safe and clean water. By leveraging predictive modeling, authorities can optimize water composition, treatment methods, and contamination control to ensure consistent water quality. This analysis would provide an innovative solution to addressing water safety issues, reducing the risk of public health hazards from contaminated water sources. Ultimately, we can lower the costs associated with manual testing, improve public health outcomes, and enhance the efficiency of water resource management systems.

Building the pipeline¶

Scaling: We are using the StandardScaler to standardize our dataset. Standardization helps ensure that each feature contributes equally to the analysis, improving the performance of subsequent models, especially those sensitive to feature scaling.

Feature Selection: We have used ANOVA F-statistic scores and Mutual Information (MI) scores for feature selection to understand the association between features and the target variable. A large F-score with a small p-value indicates a strong linear relationship and statistical significance, suggesting that the feature is likely important for prediction. A large MI score complements this by capturing the non-linear dependencies. This combination helps ensure that selected features are both statistically significant and informative.

Feature Reduction: We are using Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to reduce the features in our dataset while retaining 90% of the variance. This method is particularly beneficial because it helps eliminate redundant and collinear features, simplifying the model without losing critical information. By focusing on the most significant components, we can improve the model performance.

# Custom Transformer for Feature Selection
class FeatureSelector(BaseEstimator, TransformerMixin):
    def __init__(self, f_threshold=0.2, p_threshold=0.001, mi_threshold=0.2):
        self.f_threshold = f_threshold
        self.p_threshold = p_threshold
        self.mi_threshold = mi_threshold

    def fit(self, X, y):
        f, p = f_classif(X, y)
        f /= np.max(f)
        self.mi = mutual_info_classif(X, y)
        self.mi /= np.max(self.mi)

        # visualizing threshold for f score
        plt.figure()
        plt.plot(f, 'o')
        plt.plot(0.2*np.ones(len(f)))
        plt.ylabel('f score')
        plt.xlabel('features')
        
        # visualizing threshold for mi score
        plt.figure()
        plt.plot(self.mi, 'o')
        plt.plot(0.2*np.ones(len(self.mi)))
        plt.ylabel('mi score')
        plt.xlabel('features')

        # create mask
        self.feature_mask = ((f > self.f_threshold) & (p < self.p_threshold)) | (self.mi > self.mi_threshold)
        return self

    def transform(self, X):
        return X[:, self.feature_mask]

Best Preprocessing Sequence¶

The preprocessing steps we have chosen are as follows-

1.⁠ ⁠Outlier Detection using Isolation Forest (Implemented outside the pipeline for simplicity) --> 2.⁠ ⁠Scaling using Standard Scaler --> 3. Feature Selection using ANOVA F-statistic scores and Mutual Information (MI) scores --> 4.⁠ ⁠Feature Reduction using PCA

We believe this is the best set of sequence because -

By detecting and removing outliers before scaling, we prevent extreme values from skewing the scaling process, which could disproportionately affect the mean and standard deviation. Scaling is necessary before PCA, as PCA is sensitive to feature scaling. Features with larger magnitudes would dominate the principal components if left unscaled. Implementing feature selection next allows us to retain only the most relevant features based on their relationship with the target variable, which streamlines the dataset for more efficient processing. Feature reduction through PCA is done last, as it helps us reduce dimensionality while retaining the most important variations in the dataset only after the dataset has been cleaned and normalized. These steps should increase the likelihood of enhancing our models' generalization performance.

# Feature engineering using Polynomial features
poly = PolynomialFeatures(degree=2, include_bias=False)
X_train_poly = poly.fit_transform(X_train_final)
X_test_poly = poly.transform(X_test_final)

Polynomial features refer to the creation of new features by combining existing features through multiplication, resulting in higher-degree polynomial terms. The code uses sklearn's PolynomialFeatures with degree=2, which generates all possible combinations of the original features up to the second degree. For example, if we have features A and B, polynomial features of degree 2 would include A, B, A^2, B^2, and AB. This technique can capture non-linear relationships between features and the target variable, potentially improving the model's ability to fit complex patterns in the data. This approach expands the feature space, allowing the model to potentially discover and utilize more complex interactions between variables, which may lead to improved predictive performance.
